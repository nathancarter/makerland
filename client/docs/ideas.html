Ideas

Projects to get your creative juices flowing

Basic Behaviors

Projects you can do if you know how to attach behaviors to landscape items, movable items, and creatures

Tip: These projects are intended for use in the Sample Universe, and require only the behaviors defined in that universe initially. You can do more sophisticated projects if you add more behaviors (and you can't even do these projects at first if you started with an empty universe!).
1. New Player Tutorial

When a player first logs into the game, they appear at the point [0,0,0], and have access to only one command, Quit. (What does [0,0,0] mean?) Let's create a tutorial area that gives the player all the basic commands and explains each.

  • Teleport to "the origin," [0,0,0]. In the Sample Universe, it is the hallway where you first spawned.
  • Rework that plane to be a much longer hallway with more signs, plus some landscape items that grant the player new commands as they walk. Each command should be granted after the player has passed a sign explaining it. (Use the "Gives a player a command" behavior. Invisible square landscape items work great for this -- only makers can see them.)
  • The basic commands you should give are Settings, Inspect, Inventory, Players, Talk, Attack, Stats.
  • End with a portal that sends the player to the island.
  • Create a new character and test your tutorial area.
2. Tutorial Improvements

Take the New Player Tutorial from Idea 1 and make it more aesthetically pleasing, as follows.

  • Create new cell types and use them to create a nicer environment, so that the hallway is not just gray stone, dark stone walls, and blackness.
  • Create new landscape items and use them to decorate the hallway, so that it is not composed exclusively of square tiles, but has some decoration.
  • Upload a "new command access" sound to the Sounds table, so that players hear a pleasant sound that cues them to the receiving of each new command.
  • End the tutorial area with a portal that sends the player to the island.
3. New Maker Tutorial

Imagine you have opened your universe to a friend, and you want to promote them to be a maker. Create a tutorial area to which you could take them that will grant them all the maker commands, explaining each as it appears, just like the New Player Tutorial in Idea 1.

  • Create a new plane to house the Maker Tutorial.
  • Create a pathway with signs and command-granting landscape items, just as in Idea 1, but instead of basic abilities, grant the Maker abilities Help, Database, World, Teleport, and Reset. Be brief, and refer the readers to these very help pages!
  • At the end, don't include a portal. Have a sign tell them to use the teleport command!

To grant a player the privilege of being a maker, you as the admin visit that tutorial plane (open it in the database, click its teleport button), and bring the prospective maker to you (view the players list, click their teleport button). Then let them walk the road!

4. Beyond the Island

Create new locations on the same plane as the Island. In order to walk to them, you'll either want to extend the land of the Island itself, or add a cell type that looks appropriate, such as the wooden slats of a bridge, or some shallow wetlands over which a person could walk.

Be sure to upload several new cell types, so that you can have grassy areas, mountainous areas, and so on. Be sure to decorate them with a much greater diversity of landscape items than the one simple tree you find on the Island. (The Sample Universe is intentionally minimalist.)

5. Portals, Caves, Stairs

Rather than having your entire universe connected by walking paths, you'll want to be able to go from place to place by entering a cave, or going down stairs, or walking into a building, or stepping on a magic portal. Create some devices that teleport you (just like the portals in Ideas 1-3, above) in ways realistic to your game world. Here are some possibilities; each is a landscape item that functions as a portal to another plane, that implements the destination area:

  • A cave in the side of a mountain that sends you into an underground area. (Realistically, this should have an obvious cave exit right near where you appear, that takes you right back out.)
  • A mushroom circle that transports you to the realm of the fairies. (Getting back out might even be a puzzle or quest.)
  • Buildings that, when you step on them take you inside. (Again, an obvious exit is sensible.)
  • Inside a building, you might have staircases that go up or down, and when you step on them instantly transport you to the next floor above or below.

Not all of these need new planes. For instance, a multi-story building might be implemented all on one plane, with the floors far apart so you can't see one from the next. Players will never know.

New Behaviors

Behaviors you may want to build

(This assumes you've read the documentation on Writing Behaviors.)

Tip: These behaviors are very helpful in extending the Sample Universe to support much richer gameplay. Some of them are given to you, fully coded, in the reference documentation. You can freely copy and paste the code into your universe! Others are given as challenges, and you will need to solve them on your own.
1. Wanders randomly
Duplicate the "Wanders randomly near spawn point" behavior for creatures and rename it to just "Wanders randomly." Go through the code and remove the check that prevents the creature from walking too far from its spawn point. (No new coding required! Just copying and deleting.)
2. Is readable
To make a movable item readable, like a book or a note, you will write code very similar to that for making a creature whisper a secret. Movable items can have "uses," just like creatures do in that code.
3. Extending "Gives an item"
View the code for a "gives an item" behavior here. Can you extend it with these new features?
  • Add a new parameter for how often the player can do the action to get the item. If it's too soon, say so and don't give the item.
  • Add a new parameter for the maximum number the player can already have. If they have too many, tell them they've reached their current limit.
4. Gives a stat bonus when drunk

Duplicate the Heals/harms when drunk behavior, and instead of changing the drinker's hit points by a specified amount, change one of their stats (temporarily) by a specified amount.

This requires the function C.addStatBonus(name,amount,time) defined in living things, which increases or decreases the named stat in the creature C by the given amount (positive or negative) for the given amount of time (in milliseconds). Your new behavior will need additional parameters, the name of the stat and the duration of the bonus.

5. Gives a status conditio when drunk

Same as Idea 4, except give a "status condition". This is a message that temporarily appears beneath the player's name, in the top right of the game view (just to the left of the commands pane). It's useful for telling the player things like, "You feel a surge of strength from your energy drink!" or "That tasted terrible...Was it poison!?"

The function P.addStatusCondition(text,time) is defined in each player object P, and shows the given text (which should be short) for the given time (in seconds).

6. Spawns holding an item
Create a behavior that takes a single parameter, and index into the movable items table. Pass that index to the globally-defined Item function to construct an actual movable item from the index. Then call the move function in that item to move it to the creature that just spawned, by passing the creature as the parameter to move.
7. Extending "Spawns holding an item"
Extend Idea 6 so that it takes a second parameter, a probability between 0 and 1, and only gives the creature the item if Math.random() is less than the given probability. Thus a creature might spawn carrying a valuable treasure only 10% of the time, for example.
8. Chases enemies
Some creatures that are aggressive may want to chase you if you fight them and then run away. Implement this behavior. Here are some functions you can call in any creature C, which may help with this idea:
  • C.getPosition() gives the creature's position, as documented here.
  • C.enemiesWithin(r) gives an array of the creature's current enemies that are within a radius of r map cells of the creature.
  • C.moveBy(dx,dy,dt) moves the creature horizontally by dx steps, vertically by dy steps, and animates that over dt seconds (which can be 0 to mean instantly).
9. Flees enemies
Copy Idea 8 and alter its logic to make the creature flee from its enemies rather than pursue them. Or, if you prefer, you can extend Idea 8 with a new parameter that specifies chase/flee, so that you can keep the code in one behavior, rather than copied into two.
10. Trades items with the player
This behavior makes a creature or a landscape item so that you can interact with it and trade one item for another. For example, maybe you can bring an old woman flowers and she'll give you a loaf of bread. Or you can put coins into a vending machine to get a soda. Let the maker specify parameters for what item the player must give, what item the player will receive, what text will be shown when they receive it, and what the name will be of the action you will add to the landscape item or creature to initiate the trade. As a starting point, see the code for Gives an item.
11. Walks a set path
The Sample Universe comes with the "Wanders near spawn point" behavior, and Idea 1 suggests an enhancement you can make to it. Consider a new behavior that takes a parameter describing a set path (in whatever format you like, such as "N N E E E E E S S W W W W") and has the creature to which the behavior is attached walk that path at some given rate. This is useful for creating creatuers that patrol an area.
12. An item that is lucky to carry
See this code for how to make an event listener for before an item is picked up. You can also use "after get" or "before drop" or "after drop" events for before/after an item is gotten/dropped. Use these to make an item that gives stat bonuses when it is picked up, and removes them when dropped. You may wish to reference the code for how equipment gives bonuses.
13. Silences its holder
This behavior makes a landscape item prevent its holder from speaking. Use the "after get" and "after drop" events described in Idea 12, together with the "before speak" event. It can prevent the speech event by returning text explaining why it's blocking the speech event, just like the "before get" event handler does in this code.

Animations

Projects you can do once you've read the help on how to code your own animations

1. Useless Firework

Create a behavior that can be attached to movable items and that makes them a launchable firework. They should have an action called "launch" that just does two things: runs the "firework" animation and destroys the firework.

Then create an animation called "firework" that just dumps a message to the JavaScript console. Create a firework with this behavior, launch it, and verify that the messages show up in your browser's console.

This is not yet a firework...we'll extend it in the next few ideas.

2. Uninteresting Firework

Extend Idea 1 as follows. When you call showAnimation(), be sure to pass as the third parameter the location of the player who launched the firework.

Change your animation to use this parameter, even if in a trivial way, such as drawing a circle or rectangle around the launching location, to be sure that your animation is capable of drawing to the canvas.

3. Ultimate Firework

Extend Idea 2 as follows. Change your animation to launch some colored particles upward from the player. Find a sound file with an explosion noise in it and add it to the game database.

Then change your behavior so that in addition to calling showAnimation(), it also calls playSound(), passing the index of your explosion sound as the only parameter. Test your firework now and verify that both the animation and the sound occur.
