Challenges

Making your universe achieve awesomeness

Easy Challenges

HAHA! There are no easy challenges; that defies the very definition of the word "challenge!" But if you're not yet up for the hard stuff, go back to the Ideas page, where there are plenty of cool things you can try to build, with hints and tips and links to reference code. Then when you've conquered the kids' table and are ready for the real deal, come back to this page.

Medium Challenges

1. Sounds

There is a list of built-in sound effects the game is ready to play, if you include sound files with those names in the Sounds table in the game database. (See here for details.) Find or create sound files for each such sound and upload them, so that your game has sound effects.

2. Quests

Come up with some quests players can do in your game universe, and implement them. Here are a few examples to help you start thinking creatively.

  • A friendly creature has lost an item and asks players to find it for him/her. Finding it and bringing it back will cause the friendly creature to trade the lost item for a reward that has some value to the players.
  • Residents of a peaceful town are being threatened by nearby aggressive monsters, and ask you to defeat them and bring back the body of the leader as evidence that they can rest safely at night once again.
  • A child or pet has wandered off into dangerous territory. You must find the lost creature, defend it from any aggressive monsters nearby, and get it to follow you home.
3. Several areas

Your game world needs to be large for it to be enjoyable for players to enter. They need to feel that there is enough for them to explore, so that they can play your game for a long time without getting bored. (No one wants to kill the same 4 bandits over and over.) Create many different areas of your world, each with its own unique style, so that players can walk a long way in your world before they hit the impassable ocean or mountain or wall or whatever it is that keeps them in the areas you've built/populated.

Hard Challenges

4. Many enemies

As players gain power, they will need stronger and stronger enemies to fight, or your game ceases to be a challenge for them. Naturally, you do not want to simply create copies of the same goblin and increasing its stats.

Create many different monsters, and populate the many areas of your game with them. Big, strong things (like dragons) should have correspondingly great stats, and small things (like beetles and caterpillars) should be trivial to defeat. Everything else should sit sensibly on the spectrum in between. The more creatures you create, the more interesting your game world will be to explore, and the longer players can play before they feel that they've "seen it all."

5. Categories of abilities

Come up with several different types of abilities (that is, categories into which abilities will fall). For example, soldier abilities, wizard abilities, and engineering abilities. (Or whatever categories you want.)

Then create abilities in each of these categories. This will be a significant investment of time in coding and testing, so roll up your sleeves! This also connects directly to Idea 6, so look ahead.

6. Ability training areas

For each category of abilities you created, build an area where there are trainers for those abilities. For example, if you created some abilities that soldiers would have, you can create an army base where there are soldiers that will train you in the abilities. Or if you created engineering abilities, there may be a school that will train players in those abilities.

It's up to you whether you want the players to have to somehow earn the abilities, through quests, puzzles, combat, exploration, experience points, or trade. Your areas should include a way for players to get new commands for each ability, and ways to train the corresponding stats for their new abilities.

Ninja Challenges

7. Settings file

Find on disk the folder in which MakerLand stores your universe. (Consider using your computer's search feature, and look for a folder with your universe's name.)

In there is a file called settings.json. Open it in your favorite text editor. Try changing some of the following settings.

  • gameTitle, which is HTML content shown above the username and password blanks on the login screen
  • gameTitleImage, which lets you put in that same folder any image that is used as the title screen image
  • privateGame, which forbids the creation of new accounts by visitors, if set to true
8. Death area

Create a new plane in your universe called the Underworld. This is where players will go when they die.

Edit the setting.json file mentioned in Idea 7, and change locationPlayersAwakeAfterDeath to a point in that new plane.

Create around that point a whole underworld that players must find their way out of before they can return to the main game world. Perhaps it should be a maze with one portal out? Perhaps there are enemies too strong to fight, that must be avoided? Perhaps there are doors that open and close randomly? It's up to you!

9. Player homes

Create an item called a "deed" that gives players permission to build their own home in the game world. It should have an action called "claim land" that only functions at locations you've pre-specified (such as on a certain plane or on a certain cell type). When the player uses that action, the following happens.

A new landscape item that looks like a house is added to the game map, with a sign out front saying whose house it is. On a separate plane set a side for the interiors of houses, some empty space is filled in with the interior of the house, just for use by that player. The house functions as a portal to the interior, and the interior has an exit that portals the player back to the exterior. The house only functions as a portal for the player who created it. The house should contain a chest where the player can permanently store their loot.
